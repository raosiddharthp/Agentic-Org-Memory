KnowledgeFlow
Enterprise Knowledge Architecture · TOGAF ADM · GCP · EU AI Act

The organisational knowledge graph is the most valuable and least governed data asset in the enterprise. It has no schema, no owner, no SLA, and no recovery plan. Left unstructured, knowledge behaves like entropy — it diffuses, degrades, and becomes irrecoverable across geographies, roles, and incentive boundaries. This is not a people problem or a methodology problem. It is a value stream obstruction with measurable downstream cost, and it has a structural, architectural solution.

KnowledgeFlow is a four-layer agentic and generative AI architecture — experience, orchestration, intelligence, and infrastructure — designed to instrument, govern, and accelerate enterprise knowledge flow at regulated-industry scale. Every agent decision is explainable. Every human checkpoint is intentional. Every access policy is auditable. Deployed on Google Cloud Platform, governed by TOGAF ADM, and compliant by structural design with GDPR and the EU AI Act.

Domain analysis

The problem is universal. The failure mode is industry-specific.

Knowledge hoarding and siloing manifest as structurally distinct failure patterns across verticals — but the root cause and the architectural remedy are invariant.

Management Consulting
Meridian Consulting Group — the anchor pattern
12,000 consultants across 40 offices and 6 continents operate as independent revenue centres. Methodology, client relationship intelligence, and engagement learnings are treated as personal competitive assets — not organisational ones. Knowledge graphs are implicit, unstructured, and non-transferable. Institutional memory is a function of tenure, not system design.
Cost vector: Billable-hour duplication, onboarding latency, methodology rework, and client relationship concentration risk. A single partner departure can place $4–8M in annual recurring revenue at structural risk with no architectural mitigation.
Healthcare & Pharma
Clinical and R&D knowledge bounded by system topology
Diagnostic protocols, pharmacovigilance learnings, and trial outcomes are distributed across departmental systems with no shared ontology and no cross-boundary retrieval capability. In pharma R&D, historical negative results are routinely lost — teams re-run failed hypotheses because the failure data was never surfaced or structured.
Cost vector: 46% of product development delays stem directly from inaccessible knowledge (Innovation Research Interchange, 2023). R&D cycle duplication routinely costs $10–50M per programme.
Manufacturing & Supply Chain
Tacit operational knowledge with no capture architecture
Process calibration heuristics, failure pattern recognition, and supplier negotiation intelligence exist exclusively as tacit knowledge in the minds of senior engineers. There is no capture mechanism, no transfer protocol, and no recovery path when those individuals retire. The knowledge graph of a 30-year engineer is not recoverable post-departure.
Cost vector: 30% of senior engineers projected to retire within 5 years (Society of Manufacturing Engineers, 2023). Each departure is a permanent, unrecoverable node deletion from the organisational knowledge graph.
Technology & Software
Architecture decisions undocumented, context non-persistent
System design rationale, failure post-mortems, and dependency context live in ephemeral communication channels — Slack threads, meeting recordings, individual memory. Architecture decisions are made without access to the full decision history. Onboarding new engineers requires months of tribal knowledge transfer that has no structured equivalent.
Cost vector: 30% of developers encounter knowledge silos adversely affecting delivery 10+ times weekly (StackOverflow, 2024). Engineering velocity loss from context reconstruction is real but unmeasured — which makes it structurally invisible to leadership.

Architecture Decision Records

Six ADRs — context, decision, consequences, rebuttal

Each ADR records a consequential architectural decision, the context that made it necessary, the decision taken, its consequences, and the strongest challenge to it with a structured response.

ADR-001Neo4j Aura Enterprise as the knowledge graph store over a relational or document modelAccepted▼
Context
Enterprise knowledge is fundamentally relational. A relational schema requires join tables that grow exponentially with relationship cardinality. A document store loses relationship structure entirely. The access control model requires policy evaluation against node attributes and edge traversals simultaneously.
Decision
Adopt Neo4j Aura Enterprise as the primary knowledge store. Model all entities as nodes with typed properties and all relationships as first-class edges with direction and weight. Use Cypher for all graph queries. Deploy via GCP Marketplace for unified billing and VPC peering.
Consequences
Positive: native graph traversal, relationship-aware retrieval, GDPR-compliant managed service, no impedance mismatch. Negative: team requires Cypher proficiency; Neo4j adds a managed service cost line; graph schema migrations require careful versioning.
Rebuttal & response
"PostgreSQL with pgvector can do both relational and vector retrieval in one system." Accepted — but pgvector is a retrieval optimisation, not a graph model. Multi-hop relationship traversal with attribute-level policy filtering is not a retrieval problem. It is a graph problem. The impedance mismatch cost of modelling graph semantics in a relational schema is borne at query time, at scale, with latency consequences that are not acceptable in an interactive system.
ADR-002Composable RBAC + ABAC + OPA policy engine over a single-model access control approachAccepted▼
Context
Access requirements are multi-dimensional: a user may have a role permitting domain access, a geo policy excluding a specific office's data, and a sensitivity tag restricting certain node types. No single access control model satisfies all three dimensions simultaneously. GDPR requires access decisions to be auditable and explainable. EU AI Act requires policy decisions to be documented and version-controlled.
Decision
Layer three composable access control models: (1) RBAC via GCP IAM for coarse-grained service access; (2) ABAC via attribute tags on every knowledge node — geography, sensitivity, domain; (3) OPA policy engine for fine-grained, composable, version-controlled rules evaluated at query time. All three layers must pass for access to be granted.
Consequences
Positive: policy is declarative, testable, and version-controlled; any access decision can be reproduced and audited; GDPR and EU AI Act obligations are structurally satisfiable. Negative: three-layer policy evaluation adds latency; OPA requires Rego expertise; policy testing must be part of CI/CD.
Rebuttal & response
"Three access control layers is over-engineered. IAM alone is sufficient." IAM is coarse-grained by design — it operates at the service and resource level, not the knowledge node level. A policy of "Netherlands users cannot see China office data except when tagged non-sensitive and accessed by a Partner-level role" cannot be expressed in IAM. OPA is not additional complexity — it is the only layer that can express the access semantics the business actually requires.
ADR-003LangGraph for agent orchestration topology over a single-agent or prompt-chaining approachAccepted▼
Context
A knowledge request requires multiple specialised operations: query decomposition, policy evaluation, graph retrieval, vector retrieval, synthesis, HITL routing, and audit emission. These operations have different latency, cost, and failure profiles. A single agent handling all operations in sequence is fragile, unobservable, and expensive. Prompt chaining has no state management, no retry logic, and no human intercept capability.
Decision
Adopt LangGraph on Vertex AI Agent Builder. Model the agent topology as a directed graph where each node is a typed, independently deployable function with defined input/output contracts. Human intercept points are modelled as conditional edges that route to HITL queue when confidence thresholds are not met.
Consequences
Positive: each agent node is independently testable; state transitions are observable and logged; HITL is a first-class graph construct; agent topology is version-controlled. Negative: LangGraph adds a framework dependency; graph topology requires careful design to avoid deadlocks; team requires LangGraph proficiency.
Rebuttal & response
"A simpler prompt chain would achieve the same result with less complexity." For a proof of concept, yes. For a production system handling 10,000 daily queries across 40 offices with geo-filtered access policies and GDPR audit obligations, no. The complexity in LangGraph is the complexity of the problem made explicit and governable — not incidental complexity introduced by the framework.
ADR-004GCP-native managed services over self-hosted open-source equivalentsAccepted▼
Context
Every architectural component has a self-hosted open-source equivalent: Weaviate instead of Vertex AI Search, Airflow instead of Vertex AI Pipelines, Keycloak instead of IAP, ELK instead of Chronicle. Self-hosted options offer lower per-unit cost and greater configurability. Managed services offer SLA guarantees, automatic patching, and integrated billing.
Decision
Default to GCP-native managed services for all infrastructure components. Self-hosted alternatives are only adopted when a managed equivalent does not exist or does not satisfy a specific functional requirement that cannot be waived. This principle is logged as an Architecture Principle and governs all future component selection decisions.
Consequences
Positive: reduced operational burden; SLA-backed availability; unified billing; GCP support coverage; reduced GDPR compliance surface. Negative: managed service costs are higher per unit at small scale; vendor lock-in to GCP increases with adoption depth; some configurability is sacrificed.
Rebuttal & response
"Vendor lock-in to GCP is a strategic risk." The lock-in risk is real but overstated at the architecture level. The knowledge graph model (Neo4j), the policy engine (OPA), the agent framework (LangGraph), and the IaC (Terraform) are all portable. The GCP lock-in surface is the managed compute and storage layer — which is the layer that changes least and costs most to self-manage. The risk is accepted and documented.
ADR-005Chronicle SIEM as the compliance audit backbone over BigQuery-only loggingAccepted▼
Context
GDPR requires demonstrable audit trails for all data access decisions. EU AI Act Article 12 requires logging sufficient to enable post-hoc reconstruction of AI system outputs. BigQuery can store logs but is not purpose-built for security forensics — query latency for complex correlated searches is high, and there is no native SIEM detection rule engine.
Decision
Adopt Chronicle SIEM as the primary audit and compliance backbone. All agent decision events, access policy evaluations, HITL intercepts, and GDPR data subject request outcomes are emitted to Chronicle. BigQuery retains analytical audit logs for Looker dashboarding. Chronicle handles forensic investigation and compliance query workloads.
Consequences
Positive: purpose-built for compliance forensics; sub-second search across petabyte-scale logs; native GCP integration; satisfies both GDPR and EU AI Act audit requirements. Negative: Chronicle adds a cost line; requires security team familiarity with YARA-L detection rules.
Rebuttal & response
"BigQuery is sufficient for audit logging." BigQuery is sufficient for audit storage. It is not sufficient for audit investigation. When a Data Protection Officer needs to reconstruct every access decision affecting a specific data subject over 18 months, the difference between a data warehouse and a SIEM is measured in hours of investigation time versus minutes. The cost of Chronicle is the cost of satisfying the DPO's obligations, not a gold-plating decision.
ADR-006Human-in-the-loop as a first-class architectural construct, not a post-deployment add-onAccepted▼
Context
EU AI Act Article 14 mandates human oversight for high-risk AI systems. Most enterprise AI deployments treat human oversight as a UI feature. This does not satisfy Article 14. Oversight must be interceptive — humans must have the ability to halt, modify, or reverse agent decisions before they take effect, not merely after.
Decision
Model HITL as conditional edges in the LangGraph agent topology. Every agent decision above a configurable confidence threshold routes to an asynchronous human review queue before execution. Reviewers receive full decision context. Approval, rejection, and modification are all first-class actions with audit records.
Consequences
Positive: EU AI Act Article 14 compliance is structural, not cosmetic; every HITL decision is auditable; reviewer feedback closes the RLHF loop. Negative: HITL introduces latency for low-confidence queries; reviewer fatigue is a real operational risk managed via the Day 2 operations model.
Rebuttal & response
"HITL at scale is operationally impractical — it creates a human bottleneck." This is the correct tension to hold. The resolution is not to remove HITL but to engineer the confidence scoring system so that the volume of HITL escalations is proportionate to genuine uncertainty — not a tax on every query. The target state is less than 5% of queries requiring HITL review, achieved through continuous model improvement. The architecture supports this trajectory; the operations model governs it.

D9 · Access control model · RBAC + ABAC + OPA

Composable access control — three-layer policy architecture

All three layers must pass for a knowledge node to be included in a query response. Each layer is independently configurable, independently auditable, and independently version-controlled. The denied-access behaviour is itself a configurable policy outcome — not hardcoded logic.

Layer 1 — RBAC via GCP IAM
Scope: Coarse-grained service and resource access. Controls which users can reach which GCP services and APIs at all.
Roles: KF_Viewer · KF_Contributor · KF_Curator · KF_Admin · KF_Compliance
Binding: Managed via Terraform. Role assignments are IaC — no manual IAM grants in production.
Limitation: Cannot express node-level or attribute-level access. Passes to Layer 2 for fine-grained evaluation.
Layer 2 — ABAC via knowledge node attributes
Scope: Every Neo4j knowledge node carries four attribute tags evaluated at query time.
Attributes: geography (ISO 3166) · sensitivity (public / internal / restricted / confidential) · domain (practice area) · owner_org (organisational unit)
Evaluation: Retrieval agent filters candidate node set by matching user attributes against node attributes before graph traversal begins.
Limitation: Cannot express complex conditional logic. Passes to Layer 3.
Layer 3 — OPA policy engine (Rego)
Scope: Fine-grained, composable, version-controlled rules evaluated at query time against the filtered candidate set from Layer 2.
Rule types: Geo-exclusion · sensitivity ceiling · cross-org restriction · time-bound access · emergency override (leadership only)
Versioning: Rego policies stored in Git. Every policy change triggers a CI test suite before deployment. Policy version logged with every access decision.
Denied-access behaviour: Configurable per policy rule — anonymise node · hide entirely · show restricted placeholder · audit log only.
Example OPA rule — geo-exclusion with role override
allow { input.user.role == "KF_Admin" } # Admins bypass geo rules
allow {
  input.user.geography != "CN"
  input.node.geography != "NL-RESTRICTED"
}
allow {
  input.node.sensitivity != "confidential"
  input.user.role != "KF_Viewer"
}
denied_behaviour := "anonymise" { input.node.geography == "NL-RESTRICTED" }
denied_behaviour := "hide" { input.node.sensitivity == "confidential" }

EU AI Act · Risk classification

KnowledgeFlow under the EU AI Act — risk tier and conformity approach

The EU AI Act establishes four risk tiers with differentiated obligations. KnowledgeFlow spans two tiers simultaneously depending on deployment context. The conformity approach is structural: obligations are satisfied by design, not by documentation retrofitted after deployment.

Unacceptable risk
Prohibited
Social scoring, real-time biometric surveillance, manipulation of vulnerable groups. Banned without exception under Article 5.
High risk — Article 6
KnowledgeFlow applies here
AI systems used in employment or access to essential services that affect individuals. KnowledgeFlow's access control and knowledge routing decisions meet this classification when they influence career-relevant information access.
Obligations: risk management (Art. 9), data governance (Art. 10), technical documentation (Art. 11), logging (Art. 12), transparency (Art. 13), human oversight (Art. 14), accuracy and robustness (Art. 15). All satisfied structurally.
Limited risk — Article 52
Also applies
AI systems that interact with humans — chatbots, generated content — must disclose their AI nature. KnowledgeFlow's NL query interface and synthesised responses fall here.
Every synthesised response carries a disclosure header identifying it as AI-generated with source attribution. This is a UI design requirement, not a filter.
Minimal risk
Baseline obligations only
General-purpose AI use, recommendation systems not affecting critical decisions. KnowledgeFlow's analytics and reporting functions fall here.

GDPR · Architectural compliance model

GDPR — six principles, six architectural responses

GDPR's six data processing principles under Article 5 are not satisfied by privacy policies — they are satisfied by architecture. Each principle maps to one or more structural controls in KnowledgeFlow's design.

Lawfulness, fairness, transparency Art. 5(1)(a)
Explicit consent captured at onboarding — stored with timestamp and version in BigQuery
Every AI-generated response discloses its nature and sources — no silent inference
Access decisions are explainable — OPA policy evaluation logged and queryable
Users can view their own knowledge node and its connections at any time
Purpose limitation Art. 5(1)(b)
Knowledge is collected and processed exclusively for organisational knowledge management — purpose is declared and enforced at ingestion
Dataplex purpose tags on every data asset — cross-purpose processing is structurally blocked
OPA rules enforce that knowledge nodes tagged for one purpose cannot be retrieved in a different context
Data minimisation Art. 5(1)(c)
Architecture principle: capture only what is needed — enforced at the ingestion pipeline, not at the UI layer
DLP API detects and masks PII that was not explicitly consented — not a post-processing step
Knowledge node schema enforces minimisation — no free-text fields without explicit ontology mapping
Accuracy Art. 5(1)(d)

Google's Responsible AI practices define seven properties for responsible AI systems. Each maps to a concrete architectural control in KnowledgeFlow. These are not policy statements — they are verifiable system behaviours with observable evidence artefacts.

Socially beneficial
Google RAI Principle 1 · Vertex AI Responsible AI toolkit
KnowledgeFlow's stated purpose — democratising access to organisational knowledge — is assessed against the Business Motivation Model (D1). Configurable access ensures benefit flows equitably, not only to those with existing power.
Avoid creating or reinforcing unfair bias
Google RAI Principle 2 · Vertex AI Model Evaluation · Responsible AI practices
Embedding model bias assessments are a required gate in the Vertex AI Pipeline before any model version is promoted to production. Built and tested for safety
Google RAI Principle 3 · LangGraph safety constraints · OPA policy engine
Every LangGraph agent node has a bounded tool contract — no agent has open-ended internet access or the ability to write to external systems. OPA policy engine prevents access policy violations at the infrastructure level. Chronicle SIEM detects and alerts on anomalous agent behaviour in real time.
Accountable to people
Google RAI Principle 4 · HITL architecture · Chronicle SIEM
Every consequential decision made by or with the assistance of KnowledgeFlow is attributable to a human decision-maker — either the user who issued the query or the HITL reviewer who approved, modified, or rejected the response. No decision is solely the system's. Accountability chain is complete and auditable.
Designed for privacy
Google RAI Principle 5 · GDPR Art. 5 · Cloud DLP · CMEK
Privacy is an architecture principle, not a feature. Data minimisation is enforced at ingestion. PII detection operates before graph write. Consent is the legal basis for all processing. Upholds high standards of scientific excellence
Google RAI Principle 6 · Vertex AI Model Registry · ADRs
All model selections are documented in ADRs with explicit evaluation criteria, tested alternatives, and measurable performance benchmarks. Made available for uses that accord with these principles

KnowledgeFlow's access policy engine is the mechanism that enforces principle-aligned use at runtime. OPA rules can encode permissible use cases and block misuse patterns. Explainability — all inferences are traceable
Architecture Principle 4 · Vertex AI Explainability · Source attribution
Every synthesised response identifies the knowledge nodes that contributed to it, with confidence scores per source. Vertex AI Explainability provides feature attribution for model outputs. Architectural cost argument

The right unit of measurement: cost per knowledge query

Enterprise software is commonly costed by seat or by module. Neither unit captures the value delivered by a knowledge architecture. The correct unit is cost per knowledge query — the fully-loaded cost of answering one knowledge request, including compute, storage, retrieval, synthesis, and governance. This unit enables direct comparison against the cost of the human equivalent: a senior consultant interrupted to answer the same question.

The human cost baseline: A mid-market consulting firm's average senior consultant costs $180–$240 per hour fully-loaded. A typical knowledge request — find the relevant expert, schedule a call, transfer context, document the answer — consumes 45–90 minutes of combined time across requester and respondent. Conservative cost: $135–$360 per knowledge event.

The KnowledgeFlow cost: At mid-market scale (10,000 monthly queries), the fully-loaded cost per query including all four layers is $0.11. At enterprise scale (100,000 monthly queries), it falls to $0.05. At global enterprise scale (1M monthly queries), it reaches $0.03.

The architectural case: The cost-per-query unit makes the ROI argument at scale irrefutable. It also drives the right architectural decisions — every component choice is evaluated against its per-query cost contribution, not its nominal sticker price. The cost optimisation levers below are all architectural choices, not negotiated discounts.

Cost optimisation

Six architectural levers — each bound to an ADR

Cost optimisation in KnowledgeFlow is not a procurement exercise. It is an architectural argument. Each lever is a deliberate design choice, each is bounded by a formal ADR, and each has a quantified impact on the cost-per-query unit.

Intelligent model routing
↓ 25–40% Layer 2 token cost
Route high-frequency retrieval-only agent calls to Gemini Flash and synthesis calls requiring deep reasoning to Gemini 1.5 Pro. Flash costs ~10x less per token. The LangGraph topology enables per-agent model assignment — no application changes required.
Governed by ADR-003 — LangGraph topology enables per-node model assignment
Neo4j result caching (Redis on Memorystore)
↓ 30–50% Layer 3 query cost
Frequently traversed subgraphs are cached in Redis. Cache TTL is set per node sensitivity tier — public nodes cache for 24h, restricted nodes for 1h. Cache hit rate target: 60% at mid-market, 75% at enterprise.
Governed by ADR-001 — Neo4j retrieval architecture includes caching layer
Spot VM embedding regeneration
↓ 60–70% Pipeline 1 + 2 cost
Full graph embedding regeneration runs on Spot VMs (Sunday 02:00 UTC). At $0.01/vCPU-hour vs $0.048 standard, the cost reduction on a weekly 8-hour regeneration job at 32 vCPUs is $29/week → $5.12/week — $1,243 saved annually.
Governed by ADR-004 — GCP-native managed services include Spot VM option
Tiered storage for knowledge graph cold nodes
↓ 40% Layer 3 storage cost
Knowledge nodes not accessed in 180 days are automatically migrated to a cold tier in Neo4j with archived embeddings in GCS Nearline. Access remains available but with higher latency. Lifecycle policy is IaC — configurable without code change.
Governed by ADR-001 — storage tier strategy part of Neo4j architecture decision
Per-user query budgets
↓ 15–20% Layer 2 unbounded spike risk
Apigee X enforces daily per-user query quotas by role tier. KF_Viewer: 50 queries/day. KF_Curator: 200. KF_Admin: unlimited. Protects against inadvertent cost spikes from bulk programmatic access and provides the data for the cost circuit breaker in the Economics model.
Governed by ADR-004 — Apigee X rate limiting is a GCP-native managed capability
Committed use discounts (CUD) at enterprise tier
↓ 20–37% GKE and Vertex AI compute cost
At Enterprise tier (predictable baseline workloads), 1-year CUDs for GKE Autopilot and Vertex AI compute reduce committed VM cost by up to 37%. CUDs are Terraform-managed — commitment scope, term, and coverage are version-controlled IaC decisions.
Governed by ADR-004 — GCP-native billing optimisation within managed services

HITL feedback loop — preventing reviewer fatigueHITL
HITL escalation rate is a first-class Looker KPI reviewed weekly by the MLOps lead. Target: <5% of queries (Run phase). Warning threshold: >10%. Critical: >20% — triggers immediate confidence threshold review.
Reviewer queue triaging: escalations are automatically categorised by type before entering the reviewer queue. Reviewers only see escalation types within their role scope. Cross-contamination of queue types is structurally prevented.
Fatigue detection: if a reviewer approves >95% of escalations within a 7-day window without edits or rejections, a Looker alert flags this as a potential rubber-stamping pattern. The architecture owner is notified — not the reviewer directly.
Loop closure: every HITL decision — approve, edit, reject — is labelled and stored in the feedback store. Labels feed Pipeline 3 (RLHF). Better labels → better model → lower escalation rate → less reviewer burden. A designed negative feedback loop, not an aspirational one.
SLO: queue item response time <4h business hours · escalation rate target <5% (Run) · fatigue flag review <48h

Detection: user feedback + factual challenge → P2 incident → MLOps lead investigates grounding context → affected query IDs identified in Chronicle → users notified with corrected response within 4 hours.
Retrieval bias incident: systematic under-retrieval detected for a specific geography or role group. P2 incident → MLOps lead + architecture owner → bias root cause analysis → embedding pipeline bias evaluation → model retrain → post-incident review documented as ADR appendix.
Data leakage incident: OPA policy misconfiguration permits access to restricted node by unauthorised user. P1 incident → immediate OPA rollback (8 minutes) → affected access decisions identified in Chronicle → DPO notified within 1 hour → GDPR Art. 33 breach notification assessment within 72 hours.
SLO: P1 response <30min · OPA rollback <10min · GDPR breach assessment <72h · post-incident review <5 days
Disaster recovery — RTO/RPO per layerDR
Recovery objectives are defined per layer because each layer has a distinct failure mode and recovery mechanism. A single RTO/RPO for the entire system is architecturally dishonest — Layer 1 (Cloud Run stateless) recovers in minutes; Layer 3 (Neo4j graph) recovers in hours.
Failover: Cloud Load Balancer detects primary region health failure → traffic shifted to secondary (us-central1) within 60 seconds via Cloud DNS failover → GKE Autopilot in secondary spun up → Neo4j read replica promoted to primary (manual gate required).
DR test cadence: full failover drill conducted quarterly in a dedicated DR project. Drill results documented and reviewed at the following Architecture Board meeting. Any RTO/RPO miss during a drill is logged as a risk register item.
DR test cadence: quarterly · failover drill documented · RTO/RPO miss = risk register item
Knowledge graph retraining triggers and cadenceRetraining
Scheduled retraining: full embedding regeneration weekly (Sunday 02:00 UTC) on Spot VMs via Vertex AI Pipelines. Scheduled as a Cloud Scheduler job — cadence is a Terraform variable. All retraining runs are logged to the model registry with run ID, training data snapshot hash, and evaluation results.
Event-triggered retraining: three events trigger an unscheduled pipeline run: (1) drift alert from Vertex Model Monitor, (2) HITL escalation rate exceeds 15% for 3 consecutive days, (3) architecture owner manually triggers via Looker dashboard action. All three paths require a human initiation record.
Ontology change trigger: any approved addition to the graph ontology (new node type, new relationship type) triggers a scoped re-embedding of affected node types only — not a full graph retraining. Faster and cheaper than full regeneration.
Every retraining run produces a model card documenting training data, evaluation metrics, bias assessment results, and deployment decision rationale. No model is deployed without a signed-off model card.
SLO: scheduled retraining <8h · event-triggered retrain initiated <4h after trigger · model card required before deployment
Org change management — acquisition scenarioOrg change
Trigger: Meridian acquires AcquisitionCo (800 consultants, 3 offices). Architecture Board initiates an ADM Phase A cycle specifically for the knowledge integration — not a patch to the existing system, but a formal architecture increment with its own ADR set.
Graph merge assessment: AcquisitionCo's knowledge assets are assessed against Meridian's ontology. Conflicts (different domain taxonomies, overlapping node identifiers) are resolved in a schema reconciliation workshop. Reconciliation output is a formal ontology delta document approved by the architecture owner before any data migration.
Policy reconciliation: AcquisitionCo's geographies are added to the geo attribute taxonomy. New OPA rules are drafted, tested, and submitted via the standard policy change management process. No data from AcquisitionCo is accessible in the KnowledgeFlow graph until policy rules for their geo are explicitly approved and deployed.
Access re-certification: all existing Meridian OPA geo-exclusion rules are reviewed within 30 days of acquisition close. Re-certification is logged as a TOGAF ADM Phase H change request with DPO co-sign. The default position is deny-all for AcquisitionCo nodes until explicit allow rules are deployed.
SLO: ontology reconciliation <30 days · policy rules deployed before any data migration · re-certification <60 days post-close
